Heading: Review Request Automation Setup: Hire Help or DIY?
Body: DIY is completely reasonable when review-request automation is still a narrow build: one reliable completed-job trigger, one review destination, low job volume, and an owner or office manager willing to test timing, watch replies, and fix mistakes without risking meaningful reputation damage. Hiring help usually becomes the better move once the workflow depends on messy completion signals, different service types need different timing, complaint replies must route back to the right human quickly, or the team already knows the review ask matters but keeps losing nights to setup drift instead of getting it live. The real question is not whether you could build this yourself. ### Core sections
Heading: What this decision is really about
Body: Most owners are not choosing between free and expensive. They are choosing which kind of delay, risk, and cleanup burden they want to own. DIY lowers invoice cost but can burn owner or office time on trigger cleanup, timing tests, CRM visibility, and reply routing. The hard part is not message copy. It is defining which completed jobs qualify, what should suppress the ask, and who owns the next move when a customer replies with a concern. Hiring help makes sense once sloppy review asking would cost more than setup because the business already has enough completed work that missed or mistimed asks are weakening trust.

Heading: DIY review-request setup vs. hiring expert help
Body: DIY is strongest when the business has one clear definition of job completion, low enough volume that a mistake is survivable, and one straightforward review path. Hiring help becomes the better move when timing varies by service type, complaint routing matters, completion signals are messy, or the owner already knows the workflow should exist and keeps losing time to setup drift instead of getting it live.

Heading: Where DIY projects usually start breaking down
Body: The workflow often has no trustworthy definition of “done,” so the ask fires before the customer actually feels complete. A generic timing rule gets applied to every service type even when recurring work, quick repairs, and larger projects should not all be handled the same way. Then replies come in and nobody knows whether the office, technician, or owner is supposed to take over.

Heading: How to decide before you burn a month on setup
Body: Put a real value on owner or office time. Start with one clean review path instead of every reputation touchpoint. Separate prototype value from production value. Keep ownership either way so the business controls the sending numbers, CRM access, review destinations, and workflow docs whether the first version is built in-house or with setup help.

### FAQ

Heading: When should I hire someone to set up review-request automation instead of doing it myself?
Body: When completion signals are messy, timing varies by service type, complaint routing matters, or another month of setup drift costs more than the build invoice.
