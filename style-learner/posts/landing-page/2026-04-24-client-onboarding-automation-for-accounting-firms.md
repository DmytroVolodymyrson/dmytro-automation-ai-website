### Hero
Heading: Client Onboarding Automation for Accounting Firms
Body: Many accounting firms do not lose momentum because they cannot win the client. They lose momentum in the week after the client says yes. The engagement letter goes out late. The intake form is still missing three days later. Portal access has not been set up. The kickoff or tax-prep call is still waiting on a manual email thread. A partner or admin lead ends up stitching the whole process together from memory while new work keeps arriving. Client onboarding automation for accounting firms fixes that specific post-sale gap. It makes the first steps consistent, visible, and repeatable so new clients stop entering the firm through ad-hoc inbox work.

### Core sections
Heading: What onboarding automation actually handles in an accounting firm
Body: The page keeps the scope practical: engagement-letter delivery, intake forms, checklist-driven next steps, kickoff scheduling, internal handoff, client-facing reminders, and central visibility into which new clients are fully onboarded versus stuck.

Heading: Manual onboarding vs. automated onboarding
Body: The comparison section makes the tradeoff concrete. Automated onboarding means each client gets the same first steps in the right order, staff can see status centrally, reminders fire when signatures or intake steps are missing, and humans step in for exceptions instead of moving every routine task by hand. Manual onboarding means progress lives in inboxes, spreadsheets, and memory.

Heading: Is this a good fit for your accounting firm?
Body: The fit framing stays grounded. This workflow is strongest when the firm already wins enough clients that first-week post-sale discipline matters, the same onboarding steps repeat, and the bigger issue is messy handoff rather than lack of leads. It is not the right fit when the sequence is still undefined, client volume is too low to justify automation, or the team wants software to improvise accounting advice instead of just moving the process forward.

Heading: Important guardrails for accounting onboarding automation
Body: The page emphasizes secure portal and signature steps, explicit separation between onboarding and later document chasing, designing the sequence before busy season if possible, keeping accounting judgment with humans, and escalating client confusion quickly instead of hiding it behind reminders.

Heading: How the workflow usually works in practice
Body: The page walks through the flow stage by stage: the client is marked won, the right engagement and intake package goes out, missing steps trigger the next follow-up automatically, scheduling happens only after prerequisites are complete, and humans take over when the client has a blocker or unusual need.

Heading: What proof supports this page
Body: The proof framing stays disciplined. The live accounting parent and first-project page already isolate onboarding as one of the clearest workflow families. Adjacent proof comes from the generic client-onboarding page plus the published e-commerce CRM automation case study. The page does not pretend an accounting-specific onboarding case study is already live.
