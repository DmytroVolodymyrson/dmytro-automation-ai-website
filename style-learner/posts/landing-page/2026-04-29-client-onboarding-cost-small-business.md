### Hero
Heading: Client Onboarding Automation Cost for Small Business
Body: Most small service businesses should expect a focused client onboarding build to land somewhere between $1.5K and $4.5K, with monthly running costs usually in the $30-$120 range depending on client volume, tool stack, intake complexity, and how much CRM or scheduling routing already exists. The right question is not just what it costs. It is what part of the onboarding problem the business is actually trying to fix. A business that only needs a reliable welcome sequence and a single intake form should not buy the same system as a business that needs multi-step document collection with chase logic, kickoff scheduling gated on intake completion, internal task ownership, CRM stage movement, and stall detection.
Sub: Below: what different onboarding builds usually cost, what those budget bands actually buy, what makes the price go up, where owners overspend, and when the economics make sense versus when a lighter manual process is still enough.

### Core sections
Heading: What client onboarding automation usually costs
Body: The page prices the onboarding layer itself — after the deal closes, before service delivery starts. It breaks cost into practical bands: welcome sequence with basic intake, intake plus document collection with chase reminders, kickoff scheduling gated on completion, internal task routing with CRM stage movement, and full onboarding builds with stall detection.

Heading: What those budget bands actually buy
Body: Lower-end builds solve welcome-sequence consistency. Mid-range builds add real intake sequencing, document collection with chase logic, and scheduling gates that wait for completion. Higher-end builds add internal task routing, team notifications, CRM milestones, stall detection, and conditional paths for different engagement types.

Heading: What makes the price go up
Body: Cost increases when the deal-won trigger is messy, multiple engagement types need different intake paths, document collection requires real branching and chase logic, kickoff scheduling must gate on intake completion, and internal tasks need assignment, notifications, and CRM visibility.

Heading: When onboarding automation is worth paying for — and when it is not
Body: The workflow is worth paying for when client volume is high enough that inconsistency matters, manual follow-through after deal close is unreliable, and a cleaner handoff would visibly improve retention or time-to-first-delivery. A lighter path is often enough when volume is low, onboarding is genuinely simple, or the real leak is still lead response before the deal closes.

Heading: How to budget this without overspending
Body: The page warns owners not to buy a broader CRM rebuild if the real issue is narrower. Start with the smallest onboarding layer that closes the handoff gap, separate build cost from ongoing tool costs, expect tuning after launch, and compare the build against the cost of dropped or delayed clients.
