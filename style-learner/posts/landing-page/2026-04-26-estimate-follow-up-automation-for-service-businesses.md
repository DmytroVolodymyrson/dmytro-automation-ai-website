Heading: What estimate follow-up automation handles after the site visit
Body: The page keeps the scope tightly on the estimate stage after an inspection or visit: a real estimate-sent trigger, stage-based reminders, messaging tied to the site visit, objection-aware follow-up, estimator-to-office handoff, and visibility into stale open estimates.

Heading: Is this a good fit for the business?
Body: The fit framing stays practical. This workflow is strongest when estimates follow inspections or site visits, too many quotes go cold, and nobody has reliable visibility into which jobs need follow-up. It is not the right next move when the bigger leak is still early lead response, when estimates usually close on the spot, or when the estimate stage itself is too inconsistent to trigger automation cleanly.

Heading: Guardrails for estimate-recovery workflows
Body: The page stresses that the estimate stage must be real before automation starts, the early follow-up should reference the actual visit, revision or negotiation questions must route to a human, and any urgency should reflect real crew, scheduling, or material constraints instead of fake pressure.

Heading: How a practical estimate-recovery workflow usually works
Body: The flow stays simple and stage-aware: the estimate is marked sent, the first follow-up lands while the visit is still fresh, later messages handle predictable hesitation, replies route back to the right estimator or office owner, and the owner finally gets a clean view of where open estimates stall.
