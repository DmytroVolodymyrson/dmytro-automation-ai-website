Body: No-show reduction sounds simple until the first reminder goes out before the booking is fully confirmed, a deposit requirement triggers on the wrong visit type, or a customer who already rescheduled keeps getting reminders for the old time. ### Core sections
Heading: What no-show reduction setup actually covers
Body: A useful build is not just “send a reminder before the appointment.” It covers the trigger that says a booking is truly real, the reminder cadence by appointment type, the reschedule path, confirmation requests that create commitment, deposit logic for higher-value visits, and post-no-show recovery once the slot is already lost.

Heading: What should be configured before launch
Body: The workflow needs appointment-type filters and exclusions, stop rules when the reply is really a complaint or service issue, CRM logging for confirmations and attendance history, and a clear recovery sequence for late cancels and actual no-shows.

Heading: When setup help is worth paying for — and when it is not
Body: Setup help usually makes sense when multiple appointment types need different timing, reschedules currently break the reminder flow, the business already knows no-show reduction matters but internal follow-through is inconsistent, or CRM/calendar integration is messy enough that the trigger layer needs real implementation work. A lighter manual path is often enough when volume is low, most bookings are same-day, or the real leak is still lead response before the appointment gets booked.

Heading: Common setup mistakes that break no-show reduction workflows
Body: The biggest failures are launching without a clean booking-confirmed signal, forcing one reminder cadence across every appointment type, forgetting to stop the old reminder sequence after a reschedule, and using deposits where they create more friction than value.
