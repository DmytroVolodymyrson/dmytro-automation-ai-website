### Hero
Heading: Referral Request Automation Setup: Hire Help or DIY?
Body: DIY is completely reasonable when referral-request automation is still a narrow build: one clean completed-job trigger, one referral ask separate from review asks, low job volume, and an owner or office manager willing to test timing, watch replies, and fix routing mistakes without losing real warm introductions along the way. Hiring help usually becomes the better move once the workflow depends on messy completion signals, different service types need different timing or different referral language, warm-intro replies must route to the right person quickly, or the team already knows the referral ask matters but keeps losing weeks to setup drift instead of getting it live. The real question is not whether you could build this yourself. It is whether post-job advocacy follow-through is important enough that you want a trustworthy referral workflow now instead of another month of inconsistent asking, half-built logic, and missed introductions.
Sub: Below: when DIY referral-request setup is still a smart move, when expert help usually pays for itself, what each path really costs, and how to tell whether you need a tutorial, a bounded setup engagement, or a simpler manual referral checklist first.

### Core sections
Heading: What this decision is really about
Body: Most owners are not choosing between free and expensive. They are choosing which kind of delay, risk, and missed-introduction burden they want to own. DIY lowers invoice cost but can burn owner or office time on trigger cleanup, referral-vs-review separation, timing tests, CRM visibility, and warm-intro routing. The hard part is not the referral message. It is defining which completed jobs qualify, what should suppress the ask, and who owns the next move when a customer replies with a real name and introduction.

Heading: DIY referral-request setup vs. hiring expert help
Body: DIY is strongest when the business has one clear definition of job completion, low enough volume that a mistake is survivable, one referral ask already separate from the review ask, and someone willing to test and tune. Hiring help becomes the better move when timing varies by service type, warm-intro routing matters, referral-vs-review separation is still tangled, or the owner already knows the workflow should exist and keeps losing time to setup drift.

Heading: Where DIY projects usually start breaking down
Body: The referral ask collapses into the review ask, weakening both. Warm-intro replies have no clear owner, so introductions cool off before anyone acts. A generic timing rule ignores service-type differences, so customers who do not yet feel the work is complete get asked to advocate too early.

Heading: How to decide before you burn a month on setup
Body: Put a real value on owner or office time. Start with one clean referral path instead of every advocacy touchpoint. Separate prototype value from production value. Keep ownership either way so the business controls the sending numbers, CRM access, referral destinations, and workflow docs whether the first version is built in-house or with setup help.

### FAQ
Heading: When is DIY referral-request setup actually the right move?
Body: Usually when the business has one clean completion signal, one straightforward referral path separate from reviews, lower job volume, and someone internal willing to test timing and maintain the workflow after launch.

Heading: When should I hire someone to set up referral-request automation instead of doing it myself?
Body: When completion signals are messy, timing varies by service type, referral-vs-review separation matters, or another month of setup drift costs more than the build invoice.

Heading: How is this different from the existing referral-request setup page?
Body: The setup page explains what good implementation includes. This page answers the earlier decision: keep DIYing, or pay for help now?

Heading: How much does it usually cost to hire help for referral-request setup?
Body: A focused build often lands around $1K-$2K when the process is already fairly clean. More complex setups with service-type timing, referral-vs-review separation, warm-intro routing, CRM visibility, and shared team ownership often land around $2K-$3K.

Heading: Can I prototype the workflow myself and then hire someone later?
Body: Yes. DIY can prove the referral gap is real. Then setup help can harden the timing, stop rules, referral-vs-review separation, logging, and introduction routing for production use.
